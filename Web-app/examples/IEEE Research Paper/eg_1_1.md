abstract

this paper presents a novel federated learning framework designed specifically for healthcare data analytics while ensuring patient privacy compliance with indian data protection regulations and hipaa standards. we propose fedhealth a system that enables multiple hospitals to collaboratively train machine learning models without sharing sensitive patient records. our approach combines differential privacy with secure aggregation protocols to provide mathematical guarantees against data leakage. experimental evaluation on real-world datasets from 5 partner hospitals demonstrates that fedhealth achieves diagnostic accuracy within 2.3% of centralized training while ensuring zero data leaves the hospital premises. the framework has been validated for diabetic retinopathy detection chest xray analysis and electronic health record based disease prediction.

keywords

federated learning, healthcare ai, privacy preservation, differential privacy, medical imaging, distributed machine learning, data protection, hipaa compliance

introduction

the healthcare industry generates massive amounts of data daily including medical images lab reports genomic sequences and electronic health records. training accurate machine learning models typically requires aggregating data from multiple sources which creates significant privacy concerns. in india alone over 1.4 billion citizens have health records scattered across thousands of hospitals and clinics. centralizing this data is not only logistically challenging but also violates privacy regulations like the digital personal data protection act 2023.

traditional approaches require data to be transferred to a central server for model training. this creates multiple risks including data breaches during transfer, unauthorized access at central repository, and legal liability for data controllers. recent incidents like the aiims delhi ransomware attack in 2022 have highlighted vulnerabilities in centralized health data storage.

federated learning offers a paradigm shift where instead of bringing data to the model we bring the model to the data. each participating hospital trains a local model on their patient data and only shares model updates (gradients or weights) with a central coordinator. the coordinator aggregates these updates to improve a global model which is then redistributed to participants.

related work and literature review

mcmahan et al introduced federated averaging algorithm in 2017 which remains the foundation for most federated learning systems. google has successfully deployed federated learning for keyboard prediction on android devices. however healthcare applications present unique challenges not addressed by these general purpose frameworks.

kairouz et al 2021 provided comprehensive survey of open problems in federated learning including systems heterogeneity statistical heterogeneity and communication efficiency. sheller et al 2020 demonstrated federated learning for brain tumor segmentation across 10 institutions showing feasibility for medical imaging.

in indian context several researchers have explored privacy preserving health analytics. gupta and singh 2022 proposed blockchain based consent management for health data. patel et al 2023 implemented homomorphic encryption for diagnostic models but reported 40x computational overhead making it impractical for real time applications.

our work differs from existing approaches by combining lightweight differential privacy with practical secure aggregation specifically designed for resource constrained hospital IT infrastructure commonly found in indian tier-2 and tier-3 cities.

methodology and proposed approach

the fedhealth framework consists of three main components

local training module deployed at each participating hospital. runs on standard hospital servers with nvidia gpu support. processes local patient data and computes gradient updates. implements client side differential privacy using gaussian noise injection.

secure aggregation protocol coordinates model updates from multiple hospitals. uses shamir secret sharing to prevent coordinator from accessing individual hospital updates. tolerates up to 30% participant dropout without training failure.

global model manager maintains the federated model. implements federated averaging with adaptive weighting based on hospital dataset sizes. handles model versioning and rollback capabilities.

the training process works as follows. first coordinator initializes global model and distributes to all participating hospitals. then each hospital trains on local data for specified number of epochs. before uploading hospitals add calibrated noise to gradients based on privacy budget epsilon. coordinator collects encrypted gradient shares and performs secure aggregation. finally aggregated update is applied to global model and new version distributed.

we implemented the framework using python 3.9 with pytorch 1.12 for deep learning components. secure aggregation uses the pysyft library with modifications for healthcare compliance. differential privacy implementation follows the opacus library with custom privacy accountant for healthcare specific risk thresholds.

experiments and results

we evaluated fedhealth on three clinical prediction tasks across 5 hospitals in karnataka including 2 government hospitals and 3 private healthcare chains.

task 1 diabetic retinopathy detection from fundus images
dataset 47832 images across all hospitals
baseline centralized accuracy 94.2%
fedhealth accuracy 92.1% with epsilon=1.0
communication rounds 150

task 2 chest xray classification for pneumonia tuberculosis and covid19
dataset 125000 images
baseline centralized accuracy 91.8%
fedhealth accuracy 89.9% with epsilon=2.0
communication rounds 200

task 3 disease risk prediction from ehr data
dataset 2.3 million patient records
baseline centralized auc 0.847
fedhealth auc 0.829 with epsilon=0.5
communication rounds 100

performance comparison shows fedhealth achieves within 2.3% of centralized training accuracy while providing strong privacy guarantees. the privacy cost measured by epsilon ranges from 0.5 to 2.0 depending on task sensitivity requirements.

communication efficiency was measured at 4.2 gb total data transfer per hospital over full training compared to 850 gb required for centralized data transfer approach. this represents 99.5% reduction in data movement.

system latency analysis shows average round time of 45 minutes including local training secure aggregation and model distribution. this is acceptable for non emergency diagnostic applications.

we also conducted membership inference attacks to validate privacy protection. attack success rate was 51.2% which is close to random guessing baseline of 50% indicating strong privacy preservation.

discussion and analysis

our results demonstrate that federated learning is viable for healthcare analytics in indian hospital settings. the accuracy gap of 2-3% compared to centralized training is acceptable trade-off for privacy benefits especially given regulatory requirements.

several challenges emerged during deployment. hospital network connectivity varied significantly with some government hospitals having only 10 mbps connections causing synchronization delays. we addressed this with asynchronous aggregation allowing faster hospitals to proceed without waiting.

computational resources at smaller hospitals were limited often only having consumer grade gpus. we implemented model compression and gradient quantization reducing local computation requirements by 60%.

data heterogeneity was significant as disease prevalence varied across hospitals. urban private hospitals had more diabetic retinopathy cases while rural government hospitals had higher tuberculosis rates. we handled this using weighted aggregation based on local dataset statistics.

conclusion

this paper presented fedhealth a practical federated learning framework for privacy-preserving healthcare analytics. our contributions include a secure aggregation protocol tolerant of participant dropouts differential privacy integration calibrated for healthcare risk thresholds and extensive evaluation on real indian hospital datasets.

the framework achieves diagnostic accuracy within 2.3% of centralized approaches while ensuring patient data never leaves hospital premises. this enables collaborative ai development across healthcare institutions while maintaining compliance with indian data protection regulations.

future work

future directions include extending fedhealth to support real-time inference for emergency diagnostics. we plan to integrate with national health stack apis for broader interoperability. investigating personalized federated learning for hospital-specific model adaptation is another avenue. we are also exploring federated learning for drug discovery and clinical trial optimization.
