deep learning models for medical image diagnostics in resource-constrained clinical settings

authors

dr. meera krishnan
department of biomedical engineering
indian institute of technology madras
chennai, india
meera.krishnan@iitm.ac.in

prof. sanjay patel
all india institute of medical sciences
new delhi, india

dr. kavitha sundaram
christian medical college
vellore, india

abstract

this paper presents a comprehensive study on deploying deep learning models for medical image diagnostics in resource-constrained clinical settings typical of indian district hospitals and primary health centres. we develop lightweight convolutional neural network architectures achieving state-of-the-art accuracy while requiring 10x less computational resources than standard models. our approach uses knowledge distillation transferring capabilities from large teacher networks to compact student models suitable for edge deployment. evaluation on datasets comprising chest xrays fundus images and dermoscopic images demonstrates diagnostic accuracy of 91.3% 93.7% and 88.9% respectively while running on raspberry pi 4 hardware. clinical validation across 8 primary health centres in tamil nadu processed 12000 patient cases with sensitivity of 94.2% and specificity of 89.1% for tuberculosis screening.

keywords

deep learning, medical imaging, convolutional neural networks, edge computing, knowledge distillation, healthcare ai, diagnostic systems, mobile health

introduction

india faces severe shortage of trained radiologists with only 12000 radiologists serving 1.4 billion population. this translates to roughly one radiologist per 100000 people compared to one per 8000 in developed countries. majority of radiologists are concentrated in urban centres leaving rural primary health centres without access to expert diagnostic capabilities.

medical imaging volumes have increased dramatically with widespread availability of digital xray machines ct scanners and fundus cameras. however these images often remain unread for days or weeks due to radiologist shortage. delayed diagnosis leads to disease progression and poor patient outcomes especially for conditions like tuberculosis where early detection is critical.

artificial intelligence and specifically deep learning has shown remarkable success in medical image analysis. models like chexnet and retinal screening algorithms have achieved performance comparable to specialists. however these models typically require high-end gpu hardware costing lakhs of rupees making them impractical for deployment in resource-constrained settings.

this paper addresses the gap by developing lightweight models suitable for low-cost edge hardware while maintaining clinical-grade diagnostic accuracy.

related work and literature review

deep learning for medical imaging gained prominence with krizhevsky alexnet in 2012. for chest xray analysis wang et al introduced chestxray14 dataset and baseline models. rajpurkar et al developed chexnet achieving radiologist-level pneumonia detection. google health demonstrated diabetic retinopathy screening in indian population with aravind eye hospital collaboration.

model compression techniques include pruning quantization and knowledge distillation. hinton et al introduced knowledge distillation where smaller student network learns from larger teacher network. howard et al developed mobilenet architecture specifically designed for mobile and embedded applications applying depthwise separable convolutions.

in indian context microsoft research india has worked on tuberculosis detection from chest xrays. qure.ai a mumbai startup provides ai radiology solutions and has processed millions of scans. remedi by manipal hospitals offers ai-assisted diagnostics. however most solutions require cloud connectivity which is unreliable in rural areas.

our work differs by focusing specifically on fully offline edge deployment using hardware available for under rs 5000 enabling sustainable deployment in primary health centre settings without internet dependency.

methodology and proposed approach

our approach consists of three stages teacher training knowledge distillation and edge optimization.

teacher model training uses standard resnet50 and densenet121 architectures pretrained on imagenet and fine-tuned on medical imaging datasets. we train separate teacher models for each diagnostic task chest xray analysis fundus screening and skin lesion classification. training uses nvidia v100 gpus with standard data augmentation and class balancing techniques.

knowledge distillation transfers knowledge from teacher to student model. student architecture uses mobilenetv3 backbone modified with attention mechanisms for medical imaging. distillation loss combines soft target loss from teacher predictions with hard target loss from ground truth labels. temperature parameter set to 4.0 based on validation experiments.

edge optimization applies additional compression techniques. post-training quantization converts float32 weights to int8 reducing model size by 4x. operator fusion combines consecutive operations reducing memory bandwidth requirements. we use tensorflow lite runtime optimized for arm processors.

the final deployment package includes optimized model files inference code patient management interface and result reporting module. entire application runs on raspberry pi 4 with 4gb ram using raspbian os. optional camera module captures images directly or images can be loaded from usb drive.

experiments and results

we evaluated our approach on three diagnostic tasks using publicly available and proprietary datasets.

chest xray analysis for tuberculosis pneumonia and covid19 detection
dataset combines chestxray14 rsna kaggle and proprietary dataset from cmc vellore totaling 180000 images
teacher model densenet121 achieved 94.1% accuracy
student model mobilenetv3 achieved 91.3% accuracy representing only 2.8% drop
model size reduced from 28mb to 3.2mb
inference time on raspberry pi 4.2 seconds per image

diabetic retinopathy screening from fundus images
dataset combines eyepacs kaggle and aravind eye hospital data totaling 95000 images
teacher model resnet50 achieved 96.2% accuracy
student model achieved 93.7% accuracy
model size reduced from 98mb to 4.1mb
inference time on edge device 3.8 seconds

skin lesion classification for melanoma and common conditions
dataset combines isic archive with proprietary indian skin type data from pgimer chandigarh totaling 45000 images
teacher model efficientnet-b4 achieved 92.3% accuracy
student model achieved 88.9% accuracy
model size 3.5mb inference time 3.1 seconds

clinical validation was conducted across 8 primary health centres in dharmapuri and krishnagiri districts of tamil nadu over 6 month period. the system processed 12000 patient cases for tuberculosis screening. results showed sensitivity of 94.2% and specificity of 89.1% compared to expert radiologist ground truth. false negative rate of 5.8% is within acceptable range for screening application where positive cases receive confirmatory testing.

user acceptance study with 24 healthcare workers showed average system usability score of 78 out of 100 indicating good usability. training time averaged 45 minutes for staff with no prior computer experience.

discussion and analysis

our results demonstrate that knowledge distillation enables deployment of clinical-grade diagnostic models on low-cost edge hardware. the accuracy trade-off of 2-4% compared to full-size models is acceptable for screening applications where the alternative is no screening at all.

several challenges emerged during field deployment. power supply instability in rural areas caused device crashes requiring uninterruptible power supply costing additional rs 3000. dust and humidity affected raspberry pi reliability requiring protective enclosures. image quality from low-cost usb cameras varied significantly impacting diagnostic accuracy.

we found that real-world accuracy was lower than test set accuracy by approximately 3% due to domain shift between training data and field conditions. this highlights importance of collecting diverse training data representing actual deployment conditions.

sustainability analysis showed total cost of ownership including hardware training and maintenance at rs 1.2 lakh over 5 years processing estimated 50000 cases. this translates to rs 2.4 per case compared to rs 50-100 for tele-radiology services making edge ai economically viable.

conclusion

this paper presented a comprehensive approach for deploying deep learning diagnostic models in resource-constrained clinical settings. our contributions include optimized knowledge distillation pipeline achieving 91-94% accuracy with 10x model compression and extensive field validation in indian primary health centres demonstrating practical feasibility.

the work enables specialist-level diagnostic capabilities in settings lacking expert radiologists addressing a critical healthcare access gap. cost-effective edge deployment eliminates dependency on internet connectivity and expensive hardware.

future work

future directions include expanding to additional diagnostic tasks like mammography and ct analysis. we plan to implement on-device learning for continuous model improvement from local data while preserving privacy. integration with national health mission screening programs is being explored. federated learning across PHC deployments could further improve model accuracy.
